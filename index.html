⚠️ DebiCheck Stays As-and-When If Thresholds Are Breached
DebiCheck policies will not become advanceable unless the policy moves onto the client's payslip (Stop Order / PERSAL). Additionally, if your NTU rate or persistency falls below the required thresholds, DebiCheck commission reverts to — or remains on — as-and-when only.

NTU threshold: 1-month NTU must be below 15%
Persistency threshold: Must be above 65%

If either threshold is breached, your DebiCheck policies will pay as-and-when until your book improves. The only way to secure advanceable commission on a DebiCheck case is to convert it to a Stop Order on the payslip. This is why the DC to Stop Order conversion strategy is so important — it protects your earnings.

How Your Commission is Calculated

First-Year Advance Formula

Monthly Premium × 12 × (75 – [Client Age + 1]) × 0.0325 × 70%

Example
Client pays R250/month, age 40.
Term = 75 – (40 + 1) = 34 years
Commission = 250 × 12 × 34 × 0.0325 × 70% = R2,296.50

Second-Year Advance

  • You earn 33.33% of the first-year commission amount as a bonus stream
  • Paid upfront once the policy crosses into its second year
  • If the policy lapses in the first 12 months of the second year, this commission is also clawed back in full

Escalation Commission

  • When a policy escalates (5% or 10% annual increase), the premium increase also earns commission
  • Always explain escalations clearly to clients so they don't lapse due to higher deductions

Clawbacks — Know the Risk

⚠️ Clawback Timeline
Months 1–6: Full clawback — 100% of your commission is recovered if the policy lapses.
Month 7 onwards: Clawback reduces monthly — you keep more the longer the client stays active.
After 24 months: No further clawbacks on first-year commission.
  • Your upfront income is not secure unless your clients stay active
  • Only clean sales, good admin, and regular client engagement will build stable income with second-year growth
  • Poor habits — over-selling or weak follow-ups — can result in negative earnings
Tips to Prevent Clawbacks
Always confirm affordability with Qlink or Stop Order approval before submitting. Explain premium escalations clearly. Follow up on all unpaid policies immediately. The more Stop Order business on your book, the lower your clawback risk.

New Advisor Onboarding Process

The full onboarding journey takes 4–6 weeks — here's exactly how it works

Onboarding is a structured process. Each phase must be completed before moving to the next. Rushing it leads to gaps in knowledge and compliance issues down the line. Trust the process.

Timeline Overview
Total duration: 4–6 weeks
In-office training → Field observation → Assessments → System provisioning → Active production

Phase 1 — In-Office Training (Days 1–3)

Your first 2–3 days are spent in the office. This is where you learn the foundation — products, processes, systems, compliance, and the Team Buffalos way of working. You will not be in the field yet.

  • Introduction to products (Value Plan, AIO, Priority Plan, ILC, Essential Med)
  • Introduction to the 3 departments and the advisor workflow
  • Introduction to IMP, Connect Me, and Santech
  • Overview of compliance — FAIS, TCF, POPIA, RPAR
  • Receive tablet and monthly data
  • Request access to the Team Buffalos Forms & Resources folder on Google Drive from your manager — all company forms are stored here: Team Buffalos Forms Folder ↗
  • Introduction to the guide — read and ask questions

Phase 2 — In-Field Observation (Days 4–10)

You go into the field with an experienced advisor or team leader to observe how everything works in practice. Watch, ask questions, and take notes. You are not presenting or collecting LOAs yet — you are learning.

  • Observe at least 2–3 facility walk-ins with an experienced advisor
  • Observe a full group presentation
  • Observe at least 2 full 1-on-1 client sittings
  • Observe a Connect Me submission after a case is captured on IMP
  • Begin noting target facilities you want to approach in your area

Phase 3 — Santech Assessments (Days 11–14)

Approximately 2 days are dedicated to completing the compulsory Santech assessments. These must be done before system access is requested. Your advisor code depends on this.

  • Log in to Santech: santech.sanlam.co.za ↗ (same login as IMP)
  • Product assessment — Value / AIO / Priority (at least 1)
  • FNA — Financial Needs Analysis
  • FICA & FAIS — minimum 80%
  • Mandates — minimum 100%
  • Annual Declaration
  • POPIA
  • TCF — Treating Customers Fairly
  • Conflict of Interest
  • Easy Pay Declaration
  • Reinstatement
⚠️ No Code = No Production
All priority assessments must be completed before system access is requested. No assessments = no code = no submissions = no commission.

Phase 4 — System Provisioning (Days 15–17)

Once assessments are completed, system access is requested. This provisioning process takes 2–3 business days. Use this time productively — continue mapping facilities, reading the guide, and preparing your first appointment list.

  • Management submits IMP access request on your behalf
  • Receive IMP login credentials (2–3 days)
  • Receive Connect Me login credentials from management
  • Log in and verify access to both systems
  • Apply for facility permits where applicable (SAPS, Corrections, SANDF) — submit to back office

Phase 5 — Active Production (Week 4 onwards)

You are now equipped and activated. Your first cases, LOAs, and submissions happen here. You work alongside your team leader until you are confident operating independently.

  • Conduct first solo facility visit (team leader or manager present)
  • Collect first LOAs
  • Submit first schedule requests
  • Conduct first solo 1-on-1 sitting
  • Submit first case on IMP and immediately capture on Connect Me
  • Complete manager spot check on first case
  • Weekly case review with manager
  • Complete remaining Santech assessments (all must be done eventually)
  • Map at least 10 target facilities for the coming month

Commercial Worksites

Opening stop order facilities at private institutions

A commercial worksite is a stop order facility opened at a private institution — allowing employee premiums to be deducted directly from their payslip, just like a government stop order. This is one of the most powerful tools available to the team and is only accessible through the sales manager or regional executive.

Who Opens a Commercial Worksite

Sales Manager & Regional Executive
Commercial worksites are opened by the Sales Manager or Jacques du Preez (Regional Executive). An advisor cannot open one independently. If you identify a private company that would benefit from this, escalate to your manager immediately.

How Quickly Can It Be Done

If the company is on board and all stakeholders are aligned, a commercial worksite can be concluded and the facility opened within 2–3 business days. Speed of execution depends on cooperation from the employer's HR and payroll team.

Why Companies Say Yes

🏢 Employee Benefit

The employer can present the stop order facility as an added benefit to their staff — access to affordable funeral and life cover directly from their payslip. This enhances the company's benefits package at no cost to the employer.

👥 Employee Retention

Employees who feel financially supported are more loyal. Access to cover through the workplace reduces financial stress and improves productivity — a genuine retention tool for the company.

💔 Solving the Real Problem

One of the most common situations employers face: an employee loses a family member and comes to the employer asking for a loan or donation because they had no funeral cover. A commercial worksite solves this problem before it happens.

💰 Company Kickback

The company receives a kickback of up to 2.5% on all premiums collected per month. This is a direct financial incentive for the employer to support and maintain the facility.

The Pitch to the Employer
"We can set up a stop order facility on your payroll at no cost to you or your company. Your employees get access to life and funeral cover deducted from their salary — reliable, affordable, and automatic. You get a company benefit that costs you nothing, helps retain your staff, and earns you up to 2.5% back on all premiums paid every month. And the next time an employee comes to you after a family death — they'll have a policy in place, not a request for help."

How to Identify a Commercial Worksite Opportunity

  • Any private company with 40 or more employees — this is the minimum threshold for a worksite to be viable
  • Farms — farm workers are an often overlooked but significant market. Farm owners who care about their workforce wellbeing are strong candidates for a commercial worksite
  • Factories, retailers, logistics companies, private hospitals, private schools, construction firms
  • Companies where employees have previously approached management for financial assistance after a death in the family
  • Companies with a payroll department that processes monthly salaries and do not currently have a group benefit scheme in place
Next Step
If you identify a potential commercial worksite, do not approach the company independently. Bring it to your manager immediately — this needs to be handled at sales manager or regional executive level to be opened correctly.

Compliance & TCF

Know the rules before you play the game

Fit and Proper Requirements

1

2

3
Class of Business Training — Must be completed within 12 months of first appointment. Completed via the Sanlam eLearning portal (Santech): santech.sanlam.co.za ↗
4

5
CPD — Continuing Professional Development — Annual hours requirement to maintain FAIS licensing. Log in and complete CPD via FA News: fanews.co.za ↗
6
Minimum Experience — A minimum of 12 months practical experience in the financial services industry is required as part of fit and proper compliance. This is tracked from your date of first appointment.

Treating Customers Fairly (TCF)

  1. Fair treatment embedded in the company's culture.
  2. Products designed for specific target markets.
  3. Clear, accurate communication before, during, and after sales.
  4. Advice is suitable and meets client needs.
  5. Products perform as promised.
  6. After-sales service is accessible and complaints are handled efficiently.

Policy Replacement Rules

  1. Record of Advice (ROA): Must clearly state why the replacement is in the client's best interest.
  2. Replacement Product Advice Record (RPAR): FSCA-mandated. Shared with both old and new insurers.
  3. Cooling-Off Period: Client has 30 days after the new policy is issued to cancel without penalty.
  4. Waiting Periods: If completed on previous policy, the new insurer must waive them — client is covered immediately for natural death.

Working Under Supervision

  • RE5 must be passed within 2 years. Class of business training within 12 months. Qualifications within 6 years.
  • Supervision reduces as competence is demonstrated and assessments are completed on Santech.

  • Savings benefits included
  • Best suited for extended family members — the troublesome family member who may be a risk (e.g. the uncle who drinks)

  • Identify needs across funeral, life, and health
  • Best combos: AIO + Essential Med; Priority + ILC
  • Use the Needs Analysis tool in IMP to build the solution

Common Gatekeeper Objections

"We've had advisors here before and it caused problems."
→ "I completely understand — can I ask what happened? I want to make sure we do this differently."

"We don't allow outside companies on the premises."

"Our staff are too busy."

Offer a lunch slot, morning briefing, or after-hours option. Show flexibility. Make it easy for them to say yes.
"We already have someone who comes here."

Sales & Production
🦬 Remember — A Presentation is a Conversation in Group Format
The moment you walk in front of a group, remember: you are not performing — you are having a conversation with multiple people at the same time. Stay calm. Speak naturally. A nervous presenter loses the room. A calm, confident advisor commands it. If you would not panic saying it to one person, do not panic saying it to twenty. Breathe. Smile. Engage. Read the room. You can present new business only, policy servicing only, or — the sweet spot — a mix of both. The approach depends on who's in front of you.

Presentation Flow

  1. Open with connection: "Good morning everyone! I'm not here to sell you new policies — I'm here to make sure you're getting the most out of what you're already paying for."
  2. Explain policy servicing: Just like a car needs a service, your policies need a check-up. Coverage, who's covered, what happens when you pass away.
  3. Highlight the benefits: Potential savings, peace of mind, streamlined claims, maximising cash back and loyalty bonuses.
  4. Introduce the LOA: It's a consent form — not a new policy. It allows us to pull your policy information and review it properly.
  5. For new business: Use real-life scenarios. Ask questions that expose gaps — who would pay for the funeral? How long would your family survive without your income?

⚠️ Collect Payslip & Bank Statement at LOA Stage
When you collect the LOA or the client's name, always request their latest payslip and most recent bank statement at the same time. Do not wait until the sitting to ask for these documents. Here is why this is critical:
  • Full needs analysis — you cannot do a proper FNA without knowing the client's income, existing deductions, and affordability
  • Full portfolio review — you need the payslip to see what is already deducted from the payslip and identify space for the new premium
  • Qlink check — you cannot run an accurate Qlink affordability check without the payslip. A failed Qlink at the sitting wastes everyone's time
  • Avoid submission errors — missing documents at the point of submission cause rejections and delays. Having everything upfront means you can submit immediately after the sitting
  • Affordability confirmation — the bank statement confirms nett income and existing debit order commitments. A client who cannot afford the premium should be identified before the sitting — not after
The Golden Rule of LOA Collection
LOA + Payslip + Bank Statement = you walk into the sitting fully prepared. No surprises. No outstanding documents. No failed Qlinks. No wasted trips. The sitting is only for closing — not for collecting what you should have already collected.

Step 9 — Client Portfolio Review

Sales & Production

The purpose of the review is to identify the problems and gaps in the client's portfolio and then sell the solution. Highlight the problem clearly — then present the fix. This is where your product knowledge and empathy come together.

Critical Things to Check in Every Review

  • Beneficiaries without cover: Who is listed as a beneficiary but has no cover of their own? Flag it — it's a gap and an opportunity.
  • Names, insured lives, dates of birth and relationships: Verify every person on every policy — full name, correct DOB, correct relationship (spouse, nephew, child, etc.). Errors cause claim delays or rejections.
  • No More Premiums Death Benefit: Identify which policies do NOT have this. Explain to the client that when they die, that policy ends — and their family loses cover. This is often the most powerful selling point. On Avbob policies, this is called the Breadwinner's Benefit — look for it on the schedule.
  • Gaps in cover: Is there enough life cover? Are all dependants covered? Is the premium-to-cover ratio reasonable?
  • Outdated policies: Policies with no escalation, no added benefits, or poor value for money.
The Formula
Highlight the problem → Show the impact → Present the solution → Close.

Capturing on IMP

  • Summary review includes all policies (Funeral, Life, Legal, Wills, RA, Savings). IMP captures life and funeral only.
  • Under Budget — click "Yes" and capture random amounts until you sit with the client.
  • Always add beneficiaries under "people in the client's life."
  • If unsure of relationship, choose "First Cousin."
  • Tick "They want their family to still have cover if something happens to them" — triggers No More Premiums Death Benefit.
  • Under Recommended Cover — always choose "No, there are some adjustments required."
  • Escalation — always choose "Premiums go up by 5% each year, cover goes up by 4%."
  • Always sign a Stop Order Mandate for every policy.

Qlink & Affordability

Before placing any policy on a government employee's payslip, check the Qlink space. Government employees are capped at 15% of basic salary for all payroll deductions.

Affordability Example
Basic salary R20,000 → 15% cap = R3,000. Existing deductions R2,200 → Space available: R800. New premium must be R800 or less.

Manual Qlink checks can be done directly on Connect Me. Use the Qlink calculator for replacement scenarios: Team Buffalos Qlink Calculator ↗

When PERSAL is Full
If the payslip is full, explore moving existing debit order policies from the payslip to the bank to free up PERSAL space. If space is still insufficient at point of sale, initiate a DebiCheck as a bridge until cancellations clear on PERSAL.

Stop Order Mandates

Sign for Every Client — No Exceptions
A Stop Order Mandate must be signed for every client. Do not date it. Do not add the premium or policy number. You will need it going forward if a conversion doesn't happen within the 3-month validity window.

Sales & Production

The 3 Requirements to Close

❤️ Love the Product

The client must believe this is the best solution for their needs.

🤝 Trust You

Trust is everything in sales. Without it, there is no sale.

🏛️ Trust the Company

The brand you represent carries weight. Use it confidently.

Closing Tone

Handling Objections

Always repeat the objection back to the client as a question first — this surfaces the real reason behind it.

"I can't afford it right now."
→ "So cost is the main concern for you right now — is that right?"
Reframe: "Think of this as protecting your family's future." → Offer alternatives: "Let's find a level of cover that fits comfortably within your budget."

ROA — Record of Advice

The ROA is your compliance and protection tool. It must document the full advice process. Always take the client through the ROA before they sign — ensure they fully understand what they are agreeing to. In a dispute, the ROA is your proof.

The ROA must capture:

  • All changes to existing or new policies — increases, decreases, lives added or removed
  • Any new waiting periods applicable due to replacements, increases, or new lives added
  • Policy alterations impacting the new policy
  • Details of policy replacements and whether replaced policies are still within their waiting periods
  • Movements of external policies not being replaced (e.g. from Stop Order to Debit Order)
  • The client's needs as identified, the product options discussed, the product chosen and its benefits
  • The client's reasoning for choosing that product
  • Confirmation that the client understood and accepted the advice
Replacement ROA Wording
"Client chose to align current and new cover amount. They chose 50% lump sum cashback payment." Note all additions, removals, increases, decreases, waiting periods, and the reason for the override.
New Business ROA Wording
"Client currently has no funeral cover and chose to take out funeral cover for [Lives Assured] with amounts of [R amounts]. They are aware of the 6-month waiting period."

Override Reason — When and Why

The Override Reason is required in the ROA when you recommend replacing an existing policy despite the system recommending the client keep it. It must be detailed and specific — not generic.

✅ Valid Override Reasons

  • Client's current policy has no paid-up benefit and they need cover to continue for their children after their death
  • Client uses public transport — Double Accident Benefit is highly relevant
  • Client had a bad service experience with previous advisor and is no longer receiving support
  • A claim was rejected on their existing policy and trust has broken down
  • Client's current cover does not match their family structure or life stage (e.g. divorced, new dependants)

❌ Invalid Override Reasons

  • "Client wants to consolidate"
  • "Client liked the Sanlam product more"
  • "Sanlam is cheaper"
  • "Simplified portfolio"

These are not acceptable. The override reason must document the exact client situation, the shortfalls of the old policy, and the exact benefit in the new product that meets the client's needs.

The Purpose of a Replacement — Why We Do It & TCF

A replacement is not done to churn business — it is done to genuinely improve the client's financial position. Before replacing any policy, you must be able to clearly answer: Is this in the client's best interest?

  • Purpose: To provide the client with better cover, improved benefits, or a more suitable product that the current policy does not offer.
  • Benefit to the client: Access to the No More Premiums Death Benefit, improved cover amounts, consolidated cover across fewer policies, cash back features, or removing outdated/underperforming policies.
  • TCF requirement: Treating Customers Fairly applies strictly to replacements. The client must fully understand what they are giving up and what they are gaining. If the replacement does not clearly benefit the client, it should not be done.
  • RPAR (Replacement Product Advice Record): The RPAR must capture all cover changes — lives insured, cover amounts, any cover reductions or increases, new waiting periods where applicable (new business cover or increased cover), the product chosen, new premiums, and all new benefits of the policy. Glossary & FAQ

Key terms every advisor must know

NTU — Not Taken Up
A policy initiated but never finalised or activated. A 4-month window exists before the case is marked NTU and closed. Follow up proactively within this window.
Persistency
The rate at which policies remain active over time. Measured over a 15-month window. A policy lapsing before 15 months can result in commission being clawed back.
Qlink
A payroll deduction service used by government employees under PERSAL. Allows premiums to be deducted automatically from salary. Subject to the 15% cap on basic salary.
PERSAL
The Personnel and Salary system used by South African government departments. The backend system through which Qlink deductions are processed.
DebiCheck
A South African payment authentication system where the client electronically approves a debit order directly with their bank before any deductions occur.
Stop Order Mandate
A signed authorisation allowing regular premium deductions directly from the client's payroll. Must be signed for every client — undated, without premium or policy number. Valid for 3 months.
LOA — Letter of Authority
A signed consent form allowing the advisor to request policy information from external insurers on the client's behalf. Not a new policy — nothing is moved.
ROA — Record of Advice
A documented record of the advice given to a client, including why a specific product or replacement was recommended. Protects the advisor against complaints and the Ombud.
RPAR — Replacement Product Advice Record
An FSCA-mandated document completed for all replacement cases. Shared with both old and new insurers. Must record all cover changes, lives insured, cover amounts, reductions or increases, new waiting periods, product chosen, premiums, and new benefits.
No More Premiums Death Benefit
A policy feature ensuring the policy continues for all remaining insured lives when the main policyholder dies. On Avbob policies, this is called the Breadwinner's Benefit.
Breadwinner's Benefit (Avbob)
Avbob's equivalent of the No More Premiums Death Benefit. Always check for this on Avbob schedules during the portfolio review.
TCF — Treating Customers Fairly
An FSCA regulatory framework ensuring fair treatment of clients at every touchpoint.
FAIS Act
Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act. Primary legislation governing financial advisors in South Africa.
FSCA
Financial Sector Conduct Authority. Regulatory body overseeing financial services providers in South Africa.
POPIA
Protection of Personal Information Act. Governs how client data is collected, stored, and used. Violations can result in fines and loss of advisor code.
FNA — Financial Needs Analysis
An assessment of the client's financial situation and needs. Captured in IMP and used to recommend appropriate products.

FICA

Client Red Flags

Identifying risk at the first meeting — every red flag is a potential lapse, cancellation, or NTU

Not every client in front of you is the right client. Recognising red flags at the first meeting protects you, your commission, and your persistency. Every one of the warning signs below increases the likelihood of a lapse, cancellation, or case that never gets taken up. Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it usually is.

⚠️ The Rule
A bad case is worse than no case. Writing business you know is at risk wastes your time, damages your persistency, and costs you commission through clawbacks. Walk away when the red flags are clear.

Behavioural Red Flags

Client is visibly intoxicated or under the influence
A client who cannot engage clearly cannot give informed consent. Do not proceed. Reschedule for another day.
Client is aggressive, dismissive, or refuses to engage properly
If they won't cooperate during the sale, they won't cooperate when a premium is missed or a cancellation is initiated. High lapse and NTU risk.
Client is being pressured or rushed by someone else present
Consent must be free and voluntary. A client signing under pressure is a cancellation waiting to happen — and a potential compliance complaint.
Client signs everything without reading or asking a single question
They may not understand what they're agreeing to. When the first deduction hits, they'll cancel immediately. Always ensure the client understands what they are signing.
Client cannot recall basic details about their own existing policies
Indicates disengagement with their finances. Higher risk of not caring whether premiums are paid or not.
Client seems distracted or disengaged throughout the sitting
If they're not present during the sale, they won't be present when it matters. Take the time to re-engage or reschedule.

Communication Red Flags

Client has no working phone
Cannot receive OTPs, confirm DebiCheck, complete the manager spot check, or be reached for pre-cancellation follow-up. A case without a reachable client is a lapse waiting to happen.
Client gives inconsistent contact details or keeps changing their number
If you can't reliably reach them now, you won't be able to reach them when a lapse occurs.
Client is evasive when asked about their income or existing cover
Evasiveness about finances is a sign of undisclosed debt, affordability issues, or non-disclosure that will surface at claim time.
Client refuses the manager spot check call
The spot check is a non-negotiable part of the process. A refusal blocks approval and signals the client may not have fully understood or agreed to the policy.

Financial Red Flags

Client earns below R5,000 nett per month
This is a hard disqualifier. Do not submit a case for a client below this income threshold.
No Qlink space available and debit order is the only option
Debit order collection runs at approximately 45%. The case is high lapse risk from day one. Explore moving existing policies off the payslip first before proceeding.
Client already has excessive deductions close to the 15% PERSAL cap
Very little room for a new premium. Any change in their financial situation will result in a rejection and a lapse.

Policy History Red Flags

Multiple lapsed or cancelled policies across different insurers visible on the review
A pattern of non-payment is a strong predictor of future non-payment. Understand the reason before proceeding.
Short-term policy history — policies that never lasted beyond a few months
Indicates a client who signs up and cancels quickly. High NTU and early lapse risk.
Client has been replaced by multiple advisors in a short period
A sign of churning — either by the client or by previous advisors. Approach with caution and ensure the replacement genuinely benefits the client.
Client has a rejected claim on record that was never resolved
Unresolved claim disputes make clients distrustful and more likely to cancel at the first opportunity.

Compliance Red Flags

Client is an intern, contract worker, or pensioner
These categories are not eligible. Do not collect an LOA or submit a case. This is non-negotiable.
Client's ID name does not match the name on their payslip or bank statement
FICA verification will fail. The case will be rejected. Do not proceed until the documents are consistent.
Client's ID is expired or damaged
An expired or unreadable ID cannot be used for FICA compliance. The client must obtain a valid ID before the case can be submitted.
Client's date of birth on the ID does not match what they verbally stated
Inconsistencies in personal details are a non-disclosure risk. Clarify before proceeding — incorrect DOBs affect premiums and claim eligibility.
Client's employment status cannot be verified — no payslip, no proof of income
Without proof of income, affordability cannot be confirmed and FICA requirements cannot be met. Do not submit.
Client is already being onboarded by another advisor
Duplicate submissions create compliance issues and damage relationships with the insurer. Always check before proceeding.
Client's existing policies show a pattern of non-disclosure
Conditions or lives not declared correctly on previous applications is a serious red flag. It suggests the client may not disclose accurately on the new application either — creating future claim risk.
Information the client provides verbally does not match their documents
Inconsistency between what is said and what is written is a sign of either confusion or deliberate misrepresentation. Clarify everything before proceeding.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Things new advisors consistently get wrong — learn them now so you don't pay for them later

Signing interns, contract workers, or pensioners
Only permanent employees qualify. Always confirm employment status before collecting an LOA.
Submitting without all FICA documents
Missing an ID, payslip, or bank statement will delay or block approval. Get all documents before you sit with the client — not after. Once a case is approved you cannot upload documents yourself.
Not capturing the case on Connect Me after IMP submission
If it's not on Connect Me, the spot check and approval won't happen. Submit on IMP — then immediately go to Connect Me. Every time, no exceptions.
Not following up on pre-cancellations
Skipping the daily pre-cancellation WhatsApp check is how lapses become terminations. Check your group every weekday morning without fail.
Splitting policies to inflate case count
Split policies in fairness to the client — not to reach a number. Splitting will be flagged. Split when it genuinely benefits the client's family structure.
Dating the Stop Order Mandate or adding the premium and policy number
Leave it undated and blank. You will need it going forward if the conversion doesn't happen within the 3-month validity window.
Accepting the first "No" from a gatekeeper
A no is a not yet. Find out the reason, address it, and come back. Polite persistence builds the biggest books.
Doing replacements as a junior advisor
Junior advisors (0–6 months) are not permitted to do replacements. Flag it to a senior advisor or manager.
Capturing incorrect personal details on IMP
A wrong date of birth, misspelled name, or incorrect relationship can cause a claim to be rejected years later. Double-check every detail.
Sharing client information on WhatsApp groups or in public
This is a POPIA violation. Never share ID numbers, bank details, or policy information in general groups or public spaces.

Your Monthly Rhythm

Think in weeks — plan in months

A productive month has a shape. When you know what each week is for, you stop reacting and start executing.

WeekPrimary FocusWhat This Looks Like
Week 1Heavy ProspectingMaximum facility visits, new walk-ins, presentations booked. Fill the LOA pipeline. Pre-cancellations daily.
Week 2Sittings & Closings1-on-1 sessions with clients from Week 1 LOAs. Close cases. Submit on IMP. Capture on Connect Me. Follow up on schedule requests.
Week 3Admin & Lapse FocusChase outstanding cancellations. Contact lapsing clients. Do a mid-month review — are you on track for 15 cases and R8,000?
Week 4Close & Plan AheadClose all outstanding cases. Final IMP submissions and Connect Me updates. Review the month. Plan facilities and targets for next month.
Mid-Month Check-In
By the end of Week 2, you should have at least 7–8 cases submitted. If you're behind, Week 3 becomes a production week too. Don't leave all 15 cases to the last week.
The Compounding Effect
A well-worked month builds the next month automatically. The referrals from Week 2 closings become Week 1 LOAs next month. Every month you do this right makes the next one easier.
Cut-Off Day — Plan, Don't Panic
On the last day of your production month, stop running and start planning. By cut-off day your submissions should already be in — not still in progress. Use cut-off day to:
  • Confirm all cases for the current month are submitted and captured on Connect Me
  • Review your LOA pipeline — who is booked for next month's sittings?
  • Map new facilities and book appointments for the opening week of the next production month
  • Identify any lapse risks or pre-cancellations that need attention going into the new month
  • Set your case and premium target for the new month with your manager
Don't run around like a headless chicken on cut-off day. The advisor who finishes strong and starts strong is the one who builds consistently — month after month. Chaos at cut-off is a sign that the month was not planned properly. Discipline at cut-off is a sign of a professional.

Ethics & Conduct

Your reputation is built daily — through your behaviour, attitude, and treatment of clients

You represent Team Buffalos in every interaction. How you conduct yourself in the field, with clients, and with gatekeepers determines not just your own success — but the team's reputation and access to every facility you work in.

"Do to others what you would have them do to you. This sums up the Law and the Prophets."

— Matthew 7:12

The Client-First Principle

  • Always recommend the best solution for the client — not for your commission
  • Educate before selling — ensure the client genuinely understands their policy
  • Never pressure a client or rush them into a signature
  • Keep their personal data secure and never share without consent (POPIA)

Replacements & Compliance Ethics

  • Replacements must be in the client's best interest — not just a better payout for you
  • Always explain both the benefits and the risks of cancelling existing policies
  • Complete the FNA, Record of Advice, and all forms truthfully
  • Never fabricate benefit comparisons or make negative remarks about other insurers
  • Present facts, not opinions. Focus on value improvements — cash back, repatriation, paid-up benefits
  • Document everything: FNA, ROA, and LOA must be completed for every case

Facility Conduct

  • Be respectful of protocols and gatekeepers at all times
  • Dress professionally and arrive prepared — first impressions are lasting
  • Never disrupt work — always ask for a convenient time
  • Show appreciation to HR and admin teams who assist you — they control your access
  • Introduce yourself to every department: kitchen, maintenance, nurses, security. The more people know you, the more referrals you earn.

Complaints & Escalations

  • If a client complains, listen first, then log the issue
  • Escalate to the right support staff immediately — Reane, Anelisa, or Carlene depending on the nature of the complaint
  • Keep the client informed throughout — don't leave them guessing
  • A complaint handled well can strengthen the relationship. A complaint ignored destroys it.

Professional Standards

  • Use the correct forms and documentation at all times
  • Be honest about waiting periods and benefit limits — overpromising damages trust and the Team Buffalos brand
  • Follow up consistently — it shows care and commitment
  • Avoid overpromising — underpromise and overdeliver
Remember
In every interaction, you are laying the foundation for referrals, renewals, and a strong personal brand. Ethics isn't a side note — it's the core of sustainable success. The best advisors are not the ones who sell the most. They are the ones their clients trust the most.
